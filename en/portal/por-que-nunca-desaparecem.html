Hecate, Lilith, Morrigan, Kali and Pombagira: Why Do Some Figures Never Disappear?

A reflection on liminality, the painful transformation and the resignification of the guardians of the passages, by Alexia Melusine.

Civilizations have changed. Empires disappeared. Religions have transformed. But Hecate, Lilith, Morrigan and Kali kept returning because they represent something that never goes away: the human encounter with transformation.

This sentence seems simple. But perhaps it hides a much deeper truth than we imagine.

Throughout history, entire peoples have disappeared. Languages ​​stopped being spoken. Kingdoms were reduced to ruins. Libraries burned. Temples were abandoned. Gods once worshiped by thousands have become footnotes in archeology books.

Still, some figures keep coming back. Not necessarily in the same way. Not necessarily with the same names. But they return.

Hecate reappears in discussions about magic and crossroads. Lilith returns to debates about female autonomy and psychological shadow. Morrigan continues to fascinate those who study war, destiny, and sovereignty. Kali continues to arouse admiration, fear and misunderstanding. And, in Brazil, the Pombagiras continue to occupy a unique space within the spiritual, religious and cultural imagination.

Why? What is it about these figures that crosses centuries, cultures and borders?

The most popular answer tends to be that they represent the "sacred feminine." But this explanation, although attractive, may be insufficient. The reality can be much more interesting.

What is Usually Said

Contemporary interpretation tends to present these figures as archetypes of feminine power. Hecate would be the lady of mysteries. Lilith, the primordial rebel. Morrigan, the sovereign warrior. Kali, the transforming destroyer. Pombagira, the force of independence and freedom.

This reading has true elements. But it also oversimplifies extremely complex spiritual and mythological characters. Because, historically, none of them emerged to be a symbol of empowerment in the modern sense of the word. In fact, the exact opposite often happened: they emerged to explain what scared, what escaped control, what could not be tamed.

The Counterintuitive Idea Few Consider

Perhaps what survived is not Hecate, Lilith, Morrigan and Kali. Perhaps what has survived is the human fear of transformation.

This hypothesis completely changes the discussion. Because it shifts the focus from entities to the human experience. When we observe these figures historically, we notice something curious: they all appear close to moments of rupture.

They arise when an order is ending, when an identity is being abandoned, when a cycle comes to an end, when a person needs to cross unknown territory. Human beings have always had difficulty dealing with these passages. That's why he created symbols to represent them. These symbols are given different names in each culture, but they often serve similar functions.

The Guardians of the Borders

There is a fundamental anthropological concept to understand this phenomenon: liminality. The term was developed by anthropologist Arnold van Gennep and later deepened by Victor Turner. Liminality is the intermediate state between one condition and another. It's not the beginning; It's not the end. It is the crossing — the moment in which someone no longer completely belongs to the past, but has not yet reached the future.

Interestingly, all these figures inhabit exactly this territory. They are not entities of stability; they are entities of the passage.

Hecate and the Crossroads

In Ancient Greece, Hecate was associated with crossing paths, spirits and places where the worlds seemed to touch. Its importance was not just in magic; it was in the fact of occupying the limits. She appeared where certainties ended: on roads, at borders, at doors, in choices. His presence reminded us of an uncomfortable truth: every decision requires a resignation. Every new direction requires leaving something behind.

Lilith and the Fear of Autonomy

Few figures have been as reinterpreted as Lilith. Today she is often presented as a symbol of female independence. However, for much of its history, it was seen as a threat. She represented that which could not be controlled, that which refused submission, that which refused to occupy the expected role. Perhaps this is precisely why it continues to arouse fascination: because every society creates its own versions of what it considers indomitable. Lilith changes form over the centuries, but continues to represent the tension between freedom and control.

Morrigan and the Truth No One Wants to Hear

Morrigan is often called the goddess of war, but this definition can be misleading. It doesn't just symbolize combat; it symbolizes the inevitability of consequences. In ancient Celtic accounts, she often appears associated with battlefields, not as a glorious warrior, but as someone who observes, announces and witnesses. Its symbolism is deeply uncomfortable: it reminds us that every victory has a cost, that every empire ends, that every glory is temporary. In an age obsessed with success, this is perhaps one of the hardest messages to accept.

Kali and the Destruction of Illusions

Few religious figures have suffered as many misinterpretations in the West as Kali. His image tends to make an impact: the skull necklace, the exposed tongue, the fierce appearance. But to reduce Kali to violence is to miss the point. In many Hindu traditions, Kali does not destroy people; it destroys illusions, severs attachments, breaks false identifications, and confronts everything that impedes spiritual growth. Its frightening aspect exists because the truth often scares.

And Where Do Pombagiras Come In?

This is precisely where a delicate question arises. In recent years it has become common to associate Pombagiras with figures such as Lilith, Hecate or Kali. The comparison can be interesting from a symbolic point of view, but it needs to be done carefully. The Pombagiras belong to their own religious universe: they are not Brazilian versions of these figures, they were not born from the same cultural matrix, they do not have the same theological function and they did not emerge from the same historical experiences.

However, there is a point of contact that deserves attention. Like the previous figures, many Pombagiras also operate in transition territories: at crossroads, at limits, in choices, in moments when a life needs to change direction.

What Is Often Omitted About Pombagiras

Much of contemporary content presents Pombagiras only as symbols of sensuality, self-esteem, independence or prosperity. These aspects exist, but they do not tell the whole story. In the oldest accounts of terreiros we find a dimension that is often forgotten: confrontation.

Often, a Pombagira's actions do not consist of fulfilling desires, but of revealing contradictions. Not in delivering answers, but in exposing illusions. Not in facilitating paths, but in showing why certain paths remain closed. This view rarely appears on social media. Maybe because it's less comfortable; perhaps because it requires responsibility; perhaps because it forces people to look at themselves.

The True Link Between Them All

If there is a common point between Hecate, Lilith, Morrigan, Kali and several Pombagiras, perhaps it is not power. Maybe it's the transformation. But not the romantic transformation that appears in motivational phrases. The real transformation — the one that hurts, the one that dismantles certainties, the one that requires abandoning old identities.

These figures appear repeatedly in human narratives because they represent moments that no one can avoid: the end of relationships, the loss of status, the symbolic death of old versions of ourselves, the abandonment of beliefs and the crossing into unknown territories. These are universal moments. And because they are universal, they continue to produce universal symbols.

A Final Reflection

Perhaps the biggest mistake is believing that these figures represent power. Because, when observed more closely, they tend to appear precisely when power is being lost, when control disappears, when plans fail, when certainties crumble, when life forces someone to cross a crossroads.

Maybe that's why they never disappear. Not because they are ancient, not because they are mysterious, not because they belong to exotic traditions, but because they continue to talk about something that remains as current as it was thousands of years ago: the moment in which a person stops being who they were and still doesn't know who they will become. It is in this uncertain space that great transformations occur. And perhaps that is exactly where we will always find Hecate, Lilith, Morrigan, Kali and the Pombagiras. Not as answers, but as questions—questions that each generation must face again.
