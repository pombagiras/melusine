Historical-Anthropological Dossier: The Twelve Pombagiras

Archetypes, Oral Memory and Disputes of Meaning in Afro-Brazilian Religions

This material has been prepared based on terreiro accounts, Umbanda and Quimbanda literature aimed at the practicing public, and academic bibliography in anthropology and the history of Afro-Brazilian religions. It seeks to present both what circulates in the terreiros and in oral tradition and what historical and anthropological research reveals — including tensions, documentary gaps, and rarely repeated readings on the subject.

The Pombagiras do not constitute a single doctrinal body: they vary from nation to nation, from terreiro to terreiro, and even from medium to medium within the same terreiro. What follows is a map of recurring trends and narratives — not a catechism.

Introduction: who are the Pombagiras

Pombagira is the name given, in Umbanda and especially in Quimbanda, to a class of female spiritual entities associated with the line of Exu. Worshipped at crossroads, cemeteries, and in the so-called "left-hand lines," they are invoked for issues of love, money, justice, protection, and opening closed paths. Each named Pombagira — Maria Padilha, Maria Mulambo, Rosa Caveira, and so many others — functions, at the same time, as an individual spiritual entity and as an archetype: a recognizable set of traits, ritual function, and repertoire of phrases that repeats, with regional variations, in terreiros throughout Brazil.

It is important to outline, from the start, a distinction that popular literature on the subject often blurs: there is a difference between Pombagira as a claimed historical figure (a woman who is believed to have existed, as in the case of Maria Padilha) and Pombagira as a spiritual and social category — an archetype that absorbs, remolds, and rereads the experiences of marginalized women, whether they are 14th-century Castilian noblewomen or prostitutes, enslaved, and liberated women of early 20th-century Rio de Janeiro.

This dossier treats the twelve entities below within this double layer: the terreiro account, as it is passed down orally and in spiritist literature, and the historical-critical reading, which situates these figures within concrete social processes — the demonization of the Orixá Exu by colonial Catholicism, the persecution of Afro-Brazilian religions, and the stigma cast upon poor, Black, prostitute, or "declassed" women who broke with expected social roles.

Counter-intuitive analysis: what the popular narrative usually inverts

1. The Pombagira is not born of evil — she is born of the punishment of deviance

The most widespread reading in popular content presents the Pombagira as a "transgressive" figure almost by aesthetic choice — seductive, rebellious, dangerous by nature. The anthropological reading inverts this emphasis: researchers like Reginaldo Prandi describe a historical process of demonization of the Orixá Exu, messenger and guardian of crossroads in the Yoruba pantheon, who was progressively associated with the Christian Devil during colonial catechesis and police persecution of macumbas in the early 20th century.

Pombagira, as the female counterpart of Exu, inherits this same operation of meaning — but with an additional layer: she carries the specific stigma attributed to women who escaped the place of wife, mother, or demure lady. In other words, Pombagira's "transgression" is not a neutral narrative choice: it is the residue of how colonial and post-colonial society framed any autonomous woman as a moral threat.

2. Maria Padilha is probably not a single historical person

Terreiro common sense often presents a closed and factual biography: Maria Padilha was born in Spain in 1334, lover and then post-humously recognized consorted queen of King Pedro I of Castile, known as "the Cruel." This narrative indeed circulates widely — but philological and historical research shows a much more tortuous and less linear trajectory than a single biography suggests.

Studies on the formation of Rio's mediumistic religiosity point out that the name "Maria Padilha" reached the Iberian imaginary as a popular magic formula between the 16th and 18th centuries — used by women in conjugal conflicts, long before any mediumistic entity called that existed in Brazil. Add to this the documented hypothesis that part of the image of a seductive "gypsy queen" associated with Maria Padilha comes not from medieval chronicles, but from the character Carmen, created by the 19th-century French writer Prosper Mérimée — a fictional gypsy whose aesthetic ended up merging with the myth.

That is: what is presented as "the true story of Maria Padilha" is, in practice, a collage of at least three distinct layers — the 14th-century Castilian court chronicle, the Iberian magic formula of centuries later, and the 19th-century romantic literary reinvention — all reorganized in urban Brazil of the early 20th century within a religious system that has its own logic and does not depend on the historical veracity of any of the three.

3. Quimbanda as "black magic" is a classification, not a neutral fact

Another poorly explained point: the separation between Umbanda (associated with charity and the "white line") and Quimbanda (associated with works considered harder, sometimes labeled "black magic" in old literature on the subject) is not a stable or unanimous doctrinal boundary among practitioners. This is largely a classification inherited from 20th-century ethnographers and folklorists who, in attempting to organize and legitimize Umbanda to the white, Christian society of the time, pushed the role of the "shadow side" onto Quimbanda — and onto Exu and Pombagira. Many contemporary Quimbanda terreiros actively reject this framing, understanding their work as justice, healing, and order — not as malice.

4. The erasure of Black women in the origin of the feminine Exu

A systematic gap less discussed in popular content: recent research in the anthropology of Afro-Brazilian religions — such as the work of journalist and researcher Claudia Alexandre on Exu-Mulher and the Nagô matriarchy — argues that the leadership of Black, enslaved, and free women in maintaining cults to Exu was historically obscured by a double masculinization: first that of Exu himself within Candomblé, then the reading that the feminine strand of the entity (the Pombagira) would be a late and secondary creation of Rio's Umbanda, and not a continuity of older female practices of spiritual resistance.

This hypothesis shifts the center of gravity of history: instead of Pombagira being just an "importation" of Iberian legends reinterpreted in Rio de Janeiro, she can also be read as part of a wider lineage of Black women who used Afro-Brazilian religiosity as a space of power and resistance in a context of slave violence and, later, post-abolition marginalization — an angle that tends to disappear when the focus falls only on the "queen of Castile."

The Twelve Pombagiras

The following sections analyze the twelve entities under a fixed structure: the central archetype, the traditional terreiro account, and the critical contrast between the common narrative and what is usually left out.

1. Maria Padilha

Archetype: Command and Royalty

Maria Padilha is, by far, the best known and most worshipped Pombagira in Brazil — so popular that her name has become almost synonymous with the category "Pombagira" itself, giving rise to numerous regional variations (Maria Padilha of the Seven Crossroads, Maria Padilha of the Cemetery, among others). The traditional account associates her with the historical figure of María de Padilla, a 14th-century Castilian noblewoman, lover and post-humously recognized wife of King Pedro I of Castile, known as "the Cruel."

In the terreiro, she is described as an entity of imposing presence, vain, demanding respect and protocol, and extremely effective in matters of love, money, and justice. Her archetype of command reflects autonomy: the Pombagira who does not ask for permission, who makes decisions, and who protects with the same intensity with which she demands loyalty.

Common narrative:

Maria Padilha was a real queen of medieval Spain, and her story is factual and well-documented.

What is usually omitted:

María de Padilla did exist, but the seductive "gypsy queen" aesthetic comes from the character Carmen (Prosper Mérimée, 19th century) and not from the 14th-century Castilian chronicle. The historical noblewoman was not of gypsy origin. Furthermore, the name circulated as an Iberian conjuring formula long before emerging as a mediumistic entity in 20th-century urban Brazil.

"The one who protects me does not sleep, and the one who owes me does not forget."

"A queen does not ask, a queen orders."

2. Maria Mulambo

Archetype: Healing and Humility

Maria Mulambo represents the direct counterpoint to the luxury of Maria Padilha: her archetype is that of transmutation through extreme humility, symbolized by the rags ("mulambos") with which she is usually represented. Oral tradition tells that she abandoned wealth or status for love, charity, or disillusionment, opting to live among the discarded.

Mediums who report her incorporation describe an energy that is both welcoming and rigorous: Mulambo welcomes those whom no one else wants, but imposes firm moral limits on those who seek help. Her work is strongly linked to the healing of deep emotional wounds and the symbolic transformation of what society discards into something of spiritual value.

Common narrative:

Mulambo is a "lesser" Pombagira of strictly charitable function, lacking the complexity or glamour of other entities.

What is usually omitted:

Popular literature ranks entities by glamour, which leaves Mulambo less explored. However, she is a central piece in Quimbanda for healing and undoing spells. Her ritual simplicity is not weakness, but rather a deliberate and strong contestation of material power.

"Where you see trash, I see luxury."

"I do not walk in trash, I transform trash into a path."

3. Maria Quitéria

Archetype: War and Protection

Maria Quitéria carries a warrior archetype, often associated in the terreiro with the Brazilian historical figure of the same name — the Bahian soldier who disguised herself as a man to fight in the Brazilian War of Independence in the early 19th century. In the spiritual field, she works in the line of Souls, linked to defense, cutting negative energies, and the vigorous protection of those who invoke her.

Mediums describe her manifestation as serious, direct, and intolerant of falsehood — a sentinel energy, not a festive one. Her work is sought after especially in situations of injustice, threat, or the need for courage to face conflicts.

Common narrative:

Maria Quitéria, the Pombagira, is the same person as the historical heroine of the Independence.

What is usually omitted:

The association between the spiritual entity and the heroine Maria Quitéria de Jesus Medeiros is a subsequent symbolic interpretation (the woman who broke gender roles to fight), functioning as an archetypal resonance, rather than a proven biographical or factual identity.

"My razor cuts evil before it arrives."

"I am not looking for a fight, but I don't run from war."

4. Rosa Caveira

Archetype: Justice and Death

Rosa Caveira works in the line of Caveira, associated with Omulu and Obaluaê, Orixás linked to disease, healing, and the territory of the dead. Her archetype is that of implacable justice and discipline: she is described as serious, of few words, and strongly linked to the theme of impermanence — the idea that death equalizes everyone, regardless of social status.

Terreiro accounts associate her with a woman who resorted to magic to protect herself from extreme adversities in life. Her ritual work is sought in cases that require a definitive cut, the closing of cycles, or justice that material life did not offer.

Common narrative:

Rosa Caveira is a purely "dark" or "evil" entity, linked to malevolent forces due to her representation of death.

What is usually omitted:

Within the cosmology of Umbanda and Quimbanda, the line of Caveira does not represent evil, but rather transmutation and karmic rigor. Death is the leveler of pride. The demonization of this figure is a direct consequence of the external Judeo-Christian filter that condemns the iconography of death in the sacred.

"Death is only the beginning of my justice."

"Underneath the skin, we are all skulls."

5. Dama da Noite

Archetype: Mystery and the Occult

Dama da Noite personifies the archetype of intuition, the guarded secret, and discreet seduction — in contrast to the more ostentamous exuberance of other Pombagiras. Mediums report a silent, observant, and selective energy that reveals itself by insinuation rather than direct speech.

Her ritual work is linked to the unveiling of hidden situations, protection against veiled betrayals, and access to the more intuitive and subconscious layers of spiritual consultation.

Common narrative:

Dama da Noite is a generic entity, without complex mysteries or significant ritual lineage in the terreiros.

What is usually omitted:

Unlike Padilha, who has consolidated origin narratives in books, Dama da Noite resists biographical fixation. Her nature lies in the very mystery of concealment, representing what the light hides and what the subconscious mind processes at night.

"What the light hides, the night reveals."

"My steps make no sound, but they leave a trail."

6. Sete Saias

Archetype: Joy and Intensity

Sete Saias represents the archetype of movement, the multiplicity of paths, and joy as an active spiritual force. The number seven symbolically refers to the seven paths or seven crossroads, indicating her ability to navigate different situations and open possibilities where there was a blockage.

Mediums who incorporate her are usually described as festive, dancing, and expansive — an energy that deliberately contrasts with the seriousness of entities like Rosa Caveira, reinforcing the idea that the pantheon is not monolithic.

Common narrative:

The joy of Sete Saias is superficial or less serious compared to more severe Pombagiras.

What is usually omitted:

In the lines of Quimbanda and Umbanda, the festive joy and the spins of Sete Saias are active tools of energy unlocking. The marginalization of her dancing expression reflects external prejudice against the ritual happiness of African matrix.

"Each skirt I spin is a path I open."

"Joy is my greatest spell."

7. Pombagira Cigana

Archetype: Freedom and Prosperity

Pombagira Cigana incorporates the archetype of nomadism, of reading destiny, and of prosperity conquered through detachment. Terreiro stories refer to gypsy clans, love sufferings, and the search for freedom — a narrative that dialogues directly with European literary representations of gypsy peoples.

Mediums who incorporate her are described as vain and magnetic, and her ritual work concentrates on material prosperity, opening financial paths, and strengthening the autonomy of those who consult her.

Common narrative:

The gypsy image of Pombagiras reflects a spiritual tradition preserved intact since Europe.

What is usually omitted:

Much of the gypsy aesthetic in Rio's Umbanda comes from 19th-century European literary romanticism (again, Mérimée's Carmen), which romanticized and exoticized real gypsy populations. Although the entity has its own strength and mystery, its visual garb reproduces stereotypes about a historically persecuted people in Europe and Brazil.

"My destiny is written in the stars, but I hold the pen."

"Gold shines, but freedom illuminates."

8. Maria Navalha

Archetype: Malandragem and the Streets

Maria Navalha is one of the Pombagiras most associated with the urban universe of Rio, linked especially to the Lapa neighborhood — a territory historically marked by prostitution, bohemian life, and social marginalization in the early 20th century. Her archetype is that of survival, street smartness, and the protection of women in situations of vulnerability.

Mediums report a strong and combative personality, who protects those on the margins and does not tolerate abuse against women. Her ritual work is sought in situations of risk, violence, or the need to cut abusive ties.

Common narrative:

Maria Navalha is a purely "marginal" figure, associated with criminality or the violent Lapa.

What is usually omitted:

Historical research on the urban regulation of Rio (1890-1930) shows that women like those who inspired Maria Navalha were criminalized by the police for the simple condition of existing autonomously outside of marriage. The entity represents the spiritual rehabilitation of women marginalized by Republican patriarchal morals.

"My razor does not wound, it liberates."

"On the street I am law, on the hill I am queen."

9. Pombagira da Praia

Archetype: Emotion and Cleansing (Water)

Pombagira da Praia symbolizes emotional fluidity, purification, and renewal, with a strong symbolic connection to Iemanjá, Orixá of the sea in Afro-Brazilian cosmology. Her energy is described as gentle, yet deep, acting especially in the clearing of old hurts, traumas, and emotional blockages.

Her ritual work usually involves cleansing baths, water offerings, and works aimed at emotional balance — a less combative and more therapeutic branch within the spectrum of Pombagiras.

Common narrative:

Pombagira da Praia is "less potent" than those of the cemetery due to her gentler profile.

What is usually omitted:

This view devalues emotional and care work (usually associated with the female gender). In ritual reality, the fluidity and transmutation of the tides controlled by Praia are high-potency tools for therapeutic relief and mental clearing.

"The sea takes what does not serve and brings what is new."

"My strength comes from the waves."

10. Maria Farrapo

Archetype: Sanity and Chaos (Mental)

Maria Farrapo represents the archetype of the reorganization of chaos and reality without filters. Terreiro narratives often involve loss of social status or episodes of deep mental suffering, finding in simplicity — or even in what society would call "madness" — an alternative form of truth and inner order.

Mediums describe a loyal, yet straight-to-the-point manifestation, without discursive detours. Her ritual work is associated with mental stability, facing difficult realities, and the courage to face what other entities might soften.

Common narrative:

Farrapo represents "madness" as a purely exotic or folkloric theme.

What is usually omitted:

The madness in Farrapo's history carries real memories of women institutionalized or abandoned due to psychiatric crises in an era without adequate support. Treating this figure requires ethical caution, understanding her as an active healer of deep psychic pain.

"In the middle of my chaos, I am the only order."

"I tear the cloth to show the soul."

11. Pombagira Menina

Archetype: Renewal and Innocence

Pombagira Menina represents spiritual youth, curiosity, and the breakdown of rigid patterns within the Pombagira pantheon itself. Terreiro accounts associate her with spirits that passed away young or who preserve youthful energy even after passing.

Mediums describe a sincere and captivating manifestation, with work focused on renewing hopes and presenting unexpected — often playful — solutions to problems that seemed locked.

Common narrative:

Pombagira Menina is a fragile figure or without significant ritual weight for serious works.

What is usually omitted:

In Quimbanda, youthful and child energies (such as erês) play a crucial role in disrupting complex spells precisely because they do not respond to adult rational logic. Her apparent lightness is a tool for breaking ritual paradigms.

"I am small in size, but giant in axé."

"Playing, I untie the knot you tightened."

12. Pombagira da Figueira

Archetype: Roots and Ancestry (Earth)

Pombagira da Figueira embodies the archetype of the ancient priestess and deep connection with the earth and ancestors. The fig tree, historically associated in various cultures with spirits and magical practices, symbolically reinforces her link with ancient herbal knowledge and pre-colonial cults.

Mediums report a strong connection with nature and ancestral knowledge in her manifestation. Her ritual work is sought for spiritual support, grounding, and restoring connection with origins — whether family, cultural, or spiritual.

Common narrative:

Figueira represents a "pure" mystical tradition isolated from colonial influences.

What is usually omitted:

Even entities with strong earth connections carry marks of syncretism with Portuguese popular Catholicism and indigenous shamanism. The search for a "pure" or untouched origin is an academic anachronism; the richness of Pombagira lies precisely in the hybrid crossing of these cultures under slave oppression.

"My roots are deep, no wind knocks me down."

"What I plant in the shadow, I harvest in the light."

Theoretical concepts and historical references

To support critical discussions and demystify the archetypes of Pombagiras, academic research resorts to classic references in anthropology and social history:

  • Reginaldo Prandi — "Exu, de Mensageiro a Diabo: Sincretismo Católico e Demonização do Orixá Exu" (Revista USP, 2001) and "Mitologia dos Orixás" — Explains the historical process of demonization of left-hand entities by police forces and colonial press in Rio de Janeiro.
  • Vagner Gonçalves da Silva — "Exu do Brasil: tropos de uma identidade afro-brasileira nos trópicos" (Revista de Antropologia, USP, 2012) — Analyzes the incorporation of the Christian devil into the figure of the Brazilian Exu and the emergence of Pombagira.
  • Claudia Alexandre — "Exu-Mulher e o Matriarcado Nagô: sobre masculinização, demonização e tensões de gênero na formação dos candomblés" — Central thesis recovering the importance of Black priestesses in cults to Exu, often overshadowed by androcentric analyses.
  • Marlyse Meyer — Folkloric research on Maria Padilha, analyzing the fusion of cabalistic, gypsy, and Iberian elements in the formation of the entity, defined as the "general jelly of Brazilian magic."
  • Margareth Rago — "Do Cabaré ao Lar: a Utopia da Cidade Disciplinar, Brasil 1890–1930" — Referential work to understand the moral regulation of prostitutes and bohemian women in Rio de Janeiro, providing the historical backdrop for figures like Maria Navalha.

Concluding remarks

The twelve Pombagiras gathered in this dossier do not exhaust the vastness of entities worshipped under this name in Brazilian terreiros — they only represent a selection of the most widespread in Umbanda and Quimbanda literature aimed at the practicing public. What unites all of them, beyond their individual archetypes, is the fact that they carry, in their symbolic origin, memories of women who broke — by choice, by circumstance, or by survival — with the social roles imposed upon them.

The purely folkloric reading of these entities, focused on spectacular biographies and aesthetics of seduction, tends to erase this denser social layer. The historical-anthropological reading, on the other hand, situates Pombagiras within concrete processes: the colonial and republican demonization of Afro-Brazilian religions, police persecution of macumbas in the early 20th century, and the systematic stigma cast upon poor, Black, and socially marginalized women. Understanding both layers — the lived faith in the terreiro and the historical process that shaped it — is what allows a truly complete informative material on the subject.
