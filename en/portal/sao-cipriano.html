Are the Pombagiras in the Book of Saint Cyprian?

A Historical Investigation into one of the Greatest Myths of Brazilian Spirituality by Alexia Melusine.

Among the countless repeated statements about Pombagiras, few are as common as this:

"All the Pombagiras came from the Book of Saint Cyprian."

The phrase appears in videos, forums, spiritual groups and even in some popular books. However, when we look at the issue from a historical perspective, we realize that the reality is much more complex — and much more interesting.

The short answer to the question that gives this article its title is: yes and no.

The modern Pombagiras did not appear within the Book of Saint Cyprian in the way they are currently known in Umbanda and Quimbanda. However, there is a deep historical relationship between Cyprian imagery, Iberian popular magic and some of the entities that would later be worshiped in Brazilian terreiros.

To understand this relationship, we need to go back a few centuries.

The First Misconception: There Is No Single Book of Saint Cyprian

When someone talks about the "Book of Saint Cyprian", they generally imagine a unique, fixed and unchanging work. Historically, this is not true.

Over the centuries, dozens of versions attributed to Saint Cyprian have emerged. Some were published in Portugal, others in Spain and many in Brazil. Certain editions are relatively small. Others have hundreds of pages. Some are close to popular religious compilations. Others clearly take the form of grimoires, bringing together spells, prayers, spells, incantations, invocations and supernatural narratives.

In other words, there is no single Book of Saint Cyprian. There is a Cyprian tradition made up of multiple versions, often modified, expanded and adapted depending on the time and place of publication. This finding completely changes the debate.

What Really Appears in These Works?

When analyzing the best-known versions of the Book of Saint Cyprian, we find several elements linked to European folk magic:

  • Encantamentos amorosos;
  • Orações de proteção;
  • Invocações espirituais;
  • Histórias de pactos;
  • Relatos sobrenaturais;
  • Práticas associadas a cemitérios;
  • Mistérios das encruzilhadas;
  • Fórmulas para atrair ou afastar influências espirituais.

Interestingly, many of these themes also appear in the symbolic universe of Pombagiras. But this does not mean that the Pombagiras were born there. It just means that both share references belonging to the same popular magical imaginary.

The Special Case of Maria Padilha

This is where the story becomes fascinating. Among the various names associated with the Book of Saint Cyprian, one of them stands out: Maria Padilha.

Contrary to what many people imagine, Maria Padilha was not born in Umbanda or Quimbanda. She was a real historical character. Maria de Padilla lived in the 14th century and was the companion of King Pedro I of Castile. His figure remained alive in the Spanish popular imagination long after his death.

As the centuries passed, his name began to appear associated with magical practices, popular sorcery and esoteric traditions of the Iberian Peninsula. Later, Maria Padilha began to appear in some versions of the Book of Saint Cyprian. It is precisely this detail that gave rise to one of the biggest historical confusions about the Pombagiras.

Three Different Marias Padilhas

A recurring error consists of treating all Marias Padilhas as if they were the same figure. In practice, we can identify at least three distinct historical layers:

  • A Maria Padilha Histórica: Uma mulher real que viveu na Espanha medieval.
  • A Maria Padilha da Magia Popular: Uma personagem que passou a integrar encantamentos, tradições esotéricas e grimórios ibéricos.
  • A Maria Padilha Espiritual: A entidade cultuada na Umbanda e na Quimbanda contemporâneas.

Although these three layers are connected, they are not identical. Over the centuries a process of symbolic fusion occurred that transformed a historical figure into a powerful spiritual archetype.

The Afro-Brazilian Origin of Pombagiras

Here we find an often ignored conclusion. The main Pombagiras currently worshiped do not have a direct verifiable origin within the Book of Saint Cyprian:

Maria Mulambo • Sete Skirts • Rosa Caveira • Lady of the Night • Queen of Souls • Queen of Cruise • Queen of Fig Tree • Girl • Gypsy

These entities emerge within Brazilian religious development, especially from the processes of formation of Umbanda, Quimbanda and other popular spiritualist expressions. In other words: they cannot be explained solely by Cyprian literature.

The Most Consistent Theory

When analyzing the formation of the Pombagiras from a historical perspective, the most solid hypothesis is that they were born from the convergence of multiple traditions:

  • Magia Ibérica: Portugal e Espanha contribuíram com grimórios, lendas, encantamentos e personagens como Maria Padilha.
  • Catolicismo Popular: Orações, santos, devoções, almas e práticas religiosas populares também exerceram influência.
  • Matrizes Afro-Brasileiras: Os cultos de ancestralidade, os Exus, as encruzilhadas e as experiências religiosas desenvolvidas no Brasil foram fundamentais para a formação das Pombagiras como as conhecemos hoje.

The result was not a simple copy of any of these traditions. It was the emergence of something new.

The Counterintuitive Conclusion

Perhaps the most surprising conclusion is this: The Book of Saint Cyprian did not create the Pombagiras.

In fact, what occurred was a process of cultural absorption. Brazilian terreiros inherited, reinterpreted and transformed certain symbols that were already circulating in European popular magic. Maria Padilha was one of these legacies. But the spiritual entity that today receives this name has become something much greater than its historical origin.

Reducing the Pombagiras to the Book of Saint Cyprian is excessively simplifying a complex religious phenomenon. The Pombagiras are the result of centuries of cultural encounters, symbolic exchanges and spiritual reconstructions carried out on Brazilian soil. Perhaps this is precisely its most fascinating characteristic. They do not belong exclusively to Africa, Europe or Brazil. They belong to the intersection of these paths. And perhaps that is precisely why they continue to arouse so much curiosity, devotion and mystery to this day.
