Where Do Pombagiras Appear in History? A Journey Through Places Books Almost Never Show

An investigation into the history of the margins, the crossroads and the figures silenced by the victors, by Alexia Melusine.

When we talk about history, most people imagine empires, kings, wars, revolutions and great characters who had their names recorded in official books. But there is another story, much less visible, that runs parallel to that taught in schools.

It's the story of the margins. The story of ordinary people. The story of the forgotten.

It is precisely in this territory that the Pombagiras seem to appear with greater strength. One of the most interesting questions a researcher can ask is: where exactly do Pombagiras appear in world history? The answer may surprise.

Contrary to what many imagine, Pombagiras rarely appear associated with major political or military events in humanity. We do not find his name in the Crusades, the Roman Empire, the French Revolution or the World Wars. But this does not mean absence; means another form of presence.

The Pombagiras inhabit a layer of history that was almost never recorded by the victors. They appear at cultural crossroads, in oral traditions, in popular cults, in feminine spiritual practices and in spaces occupied by people who hardly had a voice in official documents.

Before Brazil: The African Roots of the Archetype

One of the most common misconceptions is to believe that Pombagira was born entirely in Brazilian territory. The name is Brazilian. The organized religious form we know today as well. But many of the elements that make up the Pombagira archetype are much older.

Among peoples of Bantu and Congo-Angola origin, spiritual conceptions already existed related to crossings between worlds, ancestors, spirits linked to paths and feminine forces associated with transformation, mystery and the passage between planes. We are not talking about Pombagira as it is currently known, but about symbolic structures that, centuries later, would help form its religious identity.

Atlantic slavery brought these visions to the American continent, where they began to dialogue with other traditions.

The Forgotten Witches of the Iberian Peninsula

Long before the emergence of Umbanda, Portugal and Spain had a vast tradition of women linked to popular practices of healing, blessings and enchantment.

Between the 15th and 18th centuries, inquisitorial documents recorded numerous accusations against midwives, healers, diviners and women who claimed to work with spirits or invisible forces. Many of these women carried out work related to love, protection, fertility and the resolution of emotional conflicts. Inquisitors often described these practices as superstition, sorcery, or a demonic pact.

Although they were not Pombagiras, several scholars observe important symbolic similarities between these historical figures and the feminine archetypes that would later appear in Afro-Brazilian religions. What stands out is that these women occupied a very similar position: respected by some, feared by others and constantly marginalized by official institutions.

The Black Atlantic and the Birth of New Traditions

Perhaps one of the most important moments to understand the emergence of the Pombagiras is on the Atlantic routes of slavery.

For more than three centuries, millions of people crossed the ocean carrying languages, beliefs, memories and spiritual practices. Africans, indigenous people, Portuguese, Spanish, popular Christians, gypsies and several other groups ended up sharing experiences and knowledge. In this environment of intense cultural mixing, entirely new religious forms emerged.

The modern Pombagira can be understood as a result of this historical process. It does not belong exclusively to a single origin; it is the result of encounters, adaptations and transformations that have occurred over centuries.

Ports, Markets and Bohemian Areas: The Forgotten Scenarios

There is an aspect that is rarely discussed when talking about the history of Pombagiras. Many of the environments symbolically associated with them were spaces for the intense circulation of people and cultures: ports, markets, taverns, dance halls and bohemian regions.

Places frequented by sailors, traders, workers, freed slaves, artists, travelers and women who lived outside the social standards of the time. These places functioned as true cultural laboratories. It was in them that beliefs were mixed, symbols were reinterpreted and new forms of spirituality came to life. It is no coincidence that many images associated with Pombagiras contain elements linked to night, movement, freedom and the crossroads of human life.

The Formation of Umbanda and the Emergence of Modern Pombagira

Although its symbolic elements are very old, the religious figure of Pombagira as we know it today is relatively recent. In the first decades of Umbanda, the best-known entities were the Caboclos and the Pretos-Velhos. The Exus and the Pombagiras did not yet occupy the leading role they currently have.

Over time, especially throughout the 20th century, these entities began to gain space and recognition within different segments of Afro-Brazilian religiosity. This development is important because it shows that spiritual traditions are not static: they evolve, adapt, absorb new meanings and continue to dialogue with the needs of the communities that keep them alive.

The Women History Tried to Forget

Perhaps the most profound aspect of the history of Pombagiras relates to women marginalized over the centuries: healers, midwives, widows, independent women, sex workers and women accused of witchcraft. Women who survived in hostile contexts without the support of traditional power structures.

The automatic association between Pombagira and prostitution is a simplification that does not do justice to the complexity of the phenomenon. However, there is an important historical link. Many women who occupied positions considered inappropriate by society ended up being transformed into symbols of danger, seduction or transgression. In many spiritual contexts, these figures were later reframed as examples of resistance, autonomy and strength.

The Influence of Gypsy Traditions

Another element frequently present in some of Pombagira's lines is the influence of gypsy imagery: colorful clothing, jewelry, dance, freedom of movement and mystery. These elements were incorporated into certain religious manifestations over time. From there, figures known as Pombagira Cigana or Pombagira Sete Saias emerged. Once again, this is not a single origin, but a process of symbolic absorption that occurred throughout Brazilian cultural formation.

The Dark Feminine and International Parallels

In comparative studies on religion and mythology, some researchers observe symbolic similarities between certain characteristics attributed to the Pombagiras and female figures present in different cultures, such as Hecate, Lilith, Morrigan and Kali.

These comparisons do not mean that they are the same entity, nor do they indicate any direct relationship in origin. What exists are symbolic approximations: figures associated with the threshold between worlds, feminine power, transformation, sexuality, mystery, death and rebirth. These themes appear repeatedly in different civilizations, suggesting that certain archetypes cross different cultures and eras.

The Great Historical Contradiction of Pombagiras

There is a fascinating irony. The Pombagiras are extremely well-known within the Brazilian religious imagination, but they almost never appear in major history books. And maybe that's precisely the point: they don't belong in palaces, they belong in the streets. They do not appear in the records of the rulers, they appear in the memories of the people. They do not live in official monuments, they live in narratives transmitted orally from generation to generation.

Therefore, anyone looking for the Pombagiras only in traditional archives will find very little. But anyone who observes the markets, the ports, the crossroads, the cemeteries, the terreiros and the popular stories begins to notice something different.

Perhaps the true history of Pombagiras was never written by the victors. Perhaps it has been preserved by those whom official history has almost always left aside. And that is precisely why their study remains so fascinating: because understanding the Pombagiras is not just about studying spiritual entities; It is also about studying the memory of people, women, cultures and human experiences that survived despite the silence of official records. And perhaps it is precisely in this silence that part of its greatest mysteries still remains hidden.
