Historical-Anthropological Dossier: The Twelve Pombagiras

Archetypes, Oral Memory and Disputes of Meaning in African-based Religions

This material was prepared from terreiro reports, Umbanda and Quimbanda literature aimed at the practicing public, and academic bibliography on anthropology and history of Afro-Brazilian religions. It seeks to present both what circulates in the terreiros and oral tradition and what historical and anthropological research reveals — including tensions, documentary gaps and little repeated readings on the topic.

The Pombagiras do not constitute a single doctrinal body: they vary from nation to nation, from terreiro to terreiro, and even from medium to medium within the same terreiro. What follows is a map of trends and recurring narratives—not a catechism.

Introduction: who are the Pombagiras

Pombagira is the name given, in Umbanda and especially in Quimbanda, to a class of female spiritual entities associated with the Exu line. Worshiped at crossroads, cemeteries and on the so-called "left lines", they are invoked for issues of love, money, justice, protection and the unraveling of closed paths. Each Pombagira named — Maria Padilha, Maria Mulambo, Rosa Caveira, and many others — functions, at the same time, as an individual spiritual entity and as an archetype: a recognizable set of traits, ritual function and repertoire of phrases that is repeated, with regional variations, in terreiros throughout Brazil.

It is important to demarcate, at the outset, a distinction that popular literature on the subject often blurs: there is a difference between the Pombagira as a claimed historical figure (a woman who would have existed, as in the case of Maria Padilha) and the Pombagira as a spiritual and social category — an archetype that absorbs, re-founds and re-reads the experiences of marginalized women, be they Castilian nobles from the 14th century or prostitutes, enslaved and freed from Rio de Janeiro at the beginning of the 20th century.

This dossier deals with the following twelve entities within this double layer: the terreiro report, as passed on orally and in spiritist literature, and the historical-critical reading, which places these figures within concrete social processes — the demonization of the orixá Exu by colonial Catholicism, the persecution of Afro-Brazilian religions, and the stigma placed on poor, black, prostituted or "declassified" women who broke with expected social roles.

Counterintuitive Analysis: What the Popular Narrative Often Reverses

1. Pombagira is not born from evil — it is born from punishment for deviance

The most widespread reading in popular content presents Pombagira as a "transgressive" figure almost by aesthetic choice — seductive, rebellious, dangerous by nature. Anthropological reading reverses this emphasis: researchers such as Reginaldo Prandi describe a historical process of demonization of the orixá Exu, messenger and guardian of the crossroads in the Yoruba pantheon, who was progressively associated with the Christian Devil during colonial catechesis and the police persecution of macumbas at the beginning of the 20th century.

Pombagira, as Exu's female counterpart, inherits this same operation of meaning — but with an additional layer: she carries the specific stigma attributed to women who escaped the place of wife, mother or modesty. In other words, Pombagira's "transgression" is not a neutral narrative choice: it is the residue of how colonial and postcolonial society framed any autonomous woman as a moral threat.

2. Maria Padilha is probably not a single historical person

The common sense of terreiro often presents a closed and factual biography: Maria Padilha was born in Spain in 1334, mistress and later posthumously recognized as queen consort of Pedro I of Castile. This narrative does indeed circulate widely — but philological and historical research shows a much more tortuous and less linear trajectory than the single biography suggests.

Studies on the formation of mediumistic religiosity in Rio indicate that the name "Maria Padilha" reached the Iberian imagination as a popular spell formula between the 16th and 18th centuries — used by women in situations of marital conflict, long before there was any mediumistic entity called that in Brazil. Add to this the hypothesis, also documented, that part of the image of the seductive "gypsy queen" associated with Maria Padilha comes not from the medieval chronicle, but from the character Carmen, created by the 19th century French writer Prosper Mérimée — a fictional gypsy who has nothing to do with the historical Castilian noblewoman, but whose aesthetic ended up merging with myth.

In other words: what presents itself as "the true story of Maria Padilha" is, in practice, a collage of at least three distinct layers — the Castilian court chronicle of the 14th century, the Iberian magical formula of centuries later, and the romantic literary reinvention of the 19th century — all reorganized in urban Brazil at the beginning of the 20th century within a religious system that has its own logic and does not depend on the historical veracity of any of the three.

3. Quimbanda as "black magic" is a classification, not a neutral fact

Another little explained point: the separation between Umbanda (associated with charity and "white goods") and Quimbanda (associated with work considered harder, sometimes labeled "black magic" in ancient literature on the subject) is not a stable or unanimous doctrinal border between practitioners. It is, in large part, a classification inherited from ethnographers and folklorists of the 20th century who, in trying to organize and legitimize Umbanda before the white and Christian society of the time, pushed Quimbanda — and Exu and Pombagira — into the role of the "dark side" to be kept at a distance. Many contemporary Quimbanda terreiros actively reject this framing, understanding their work as justice, healing, and order—not evil.

4. The erasure of black women in the origin of feminine Exu

A gap that is systematically less discussed in popular content: recent research in the anthropology of Afro-Brazilian religions — such as the work of journalist and researcher Claudia Alexandre on Exu-Mulher and the nagô matriarchy — argues that the leading role of black women, both enslaved and freed, in maintaining cults of Exu was historically obscured by a double masculinization: first that of Exu himself within Candomblé, then the reading that the feminine aspect of the entity (the Pombagira) would be a late and secondary creation of Umbanda in Rio, and not a continuity of older female practices of spiritual resistance.

This hypothesis shifts the center of gravity of the story: instead of Pombagira being just an "import" of Iberian legends reinterpreted in Rio de Janeiro, she can also be read as part of a broader lineage of black women who used Afro-Brazilian religiosity as a space of power and resistance in a context of slave violence and, later, post-abolition marginalization — an angle that tends to disappear when the focus falls solely on the "queen of Castile".

The Twelve Pombagiras

The following sections analyze the twelve entities under a fixed structure: the central archetype, the traditional terreiro account, and the critical contrast between the common narrative and what is often left out.

1. Maria Padilha

Arquétipo: Comando e Realeza

Maria Padilha is, by far, the best-known and most revered Pombagira in Brazil — so popular that her name became almost synonymous with the "Pombagira" category itself, giving rise to countless regional variations (Maria Padilha da Sete Encruzilhadas, Maria Padilha do Cemitério, among others). The traditional account associates her with the historical figure of María de Padilla, a Castilian noblewoman from the 14th century, lover and later posthumously recognized as the wife of King Pedro I of Castile, known as "the Cruel".

In the terreiro, she is described as an entity with an imposing presence, vain, demanding about respect and protocol, and extremely effective in matters of love, money and justice. Her command archetype reflects autonomy: the Pombagira who does not ask for permission, who makes decisions and who protects with the same intensity with which she demands loyalty.

Narrativa comum:

Maria Padilha was a royal queen of medieval Spain, and her story is factual and well documented.

O que costuma ser omitido:

María de Padilla existed, but the seductive "gypsy queen" aesthetic comes from the character Carmen (Prosper Mérimée, 19th century) and not from the 14th century Castilian chronicle. The historical noblewoman did not have a gypsy origin. Furthermore, the name circulated as an Iberian spellcasting formula long before it emerged as a mediumistic entity in urban Brazil in the 20th century.

"Whoever protects me doesn't sleep, and whoever owes me doesn't forget."

"Queen doesn't ask, queen orders."

2. Maria Mulambo

Arquétipo: Cura e Humildade

Maria Mulambo represents the direct counterpoint to Maria Padilha's luxury: her archetype is that of transmutation through extreme humility, symbolized by the rags ("mulambos") with which she is usually represented. Oral tradition says that she abandoned wealth or status out of love, charity or disappointment, choosing to live among the discarded.

Mediums who report its incorporation describe an energy that is both welcoming and rigorous: Mulambo welcomes those who no one else wants, but imposes firm moral limits on those who seek help. His work is strongly linked to the healing of deep emotional wounds and the symbolic transformation of what society discards into something of spiritual value.

Narrativa comum:

Mulambo is a "minor" Pombagira, with a strictly charitable function, without the complexity or luxury of other entities.

O que costuma ser omitido:

Popular literature hierarchizes entities through glamour, which leaves Mulambo less explored. However, it is a central part of Quimbanda for healing and undoing the spell. Its ritual simplicity is not weakness, but rather a deliberate and strong form of contestation of material power.

"Where you see trash, I see luxury."

"I don't walk in the trash, I turn the trash into a path."

3. Maria Quitéria

Arquétipo: Guerra e Proteção

Maria Quitéria carries a warrior archetype, often associated in terreiro with the Brazilian historical figure of the same name — the Bahian soldier who disguised herself as a man to fight in the Brazilian War of Independence at the beginning of the 19th century. In the spiritual field, it acts in the line of Souls, linked to defense, the cutting of negative energies and the vigorous protection of those who invoke it.

Mediums describe its manifestation as serious, direct and little tolerant of falsehood — an energy of a sentinel, not a party. Its action is sought above all in situations of injustice, threat or the need for courage to face conflicts.

Narrativa comum:

Maria Quitéria, known as Pombagira, is the same person as the Bahian historical heroine of Independence.

O que costuma ser omitido:

The association between the spiritual entity and the heroine of Jesus Medeiros is a later symbolic interpretation (the woman who broke gender barriers to go to war), functioning as an archetypal resonance, and not as a proven biographical or factual correspondence.

"My razor cuts evil before it comes."

"I'm not one for fighting, but I don't run away from war."

4. Pink Skull

Arquétipo: Justiça e Morte

Rosa Caveira works in the line of Caveira, associated with Omulu and Obaluaê, orixás linked to diseases, healing and the territory of the dead. Her archetype is that of ruthless justice and discipline: she is described as serious, of few words, and strongly linked to the theme of impermanence — the idea that death makes everyone equal, regardless of social status.

Terreiro reports associate her with a woman who resorted to magic to protect herself from extreme adversities in life. Its ritual performance is sought in cases that require a definitive cut, closure of cycles or justice that material life did not offer.

Narrativa comum:

Rosa Caveira is a purely "dark" entity, linked to evil forces due to its representation of death.

O que costuma ser omitido:

In the cosmology of Umbanda and Quimbanda, the Skull line does not represent evil, but rather transmutation and karmic rigor. Death equals pride. The demonization of this figure is a direct consequence of the external Judeo-Christian filter that condemns the iconography of death in the sacred.

"Death is just the beginning of my justice."

"Under the skin, we are all skulls."

5. Lady of the Night

Arquétipo: Mistério e Oculto

The Lady of the Night personifies the archetype of intuition, kept secrets and discreet seduction — in contrast to the more ostentatious exuberance of other Pombagiras. Mediums report a silent, observant and selective energy, which reveals itself through innuendo rather than through direct speech.

Its ritual performance is linked to the unveiling of hidden situations, protection against veiled betrayals and access to more intuitive and subconscious layers of spiritual consultation.

Narrativa comum:

Lady of the Night is a generic entity, without complex mysteries or significant ritual lineage in the terreiros.

O que costuma ser omitido:

Unlike Padilha, who has origin narratives consolidated in books, the Lady of the Night resists biographical fixation. Its nature lies in the mystery of concealment itself, representing what light hides and the subconscious mind processes at night.

"What the light hides, the night reveals."

"My steps don't make a sound, but they leave a trail."

6. Seven Skirts

Arquétipo: Alegria e Intensidade

Sete Saias represents the archetype of movement, of the multiplicity of paths and of joy as an active spiritual force. The number seven symbolically refers to the seven paths or seven crossroads, indicating your ability to navigate different situations and open up possibilities where there was a blockage.

Mediums who embody it are often described as festive, dancing and expansive — an energy that deliberately contrasts with the seriousness of entities like Rosa Caveira, reinforcing the idea that the pantheon is not monolithic.

Narrativa comum:

The joy of Sete Saias is superficial or less serious compared to the more severe Pombagiras.

O que costuma ser omitido:

Along the lines of Quimbanda and Umbanda, the festive joy and the gyrations of Sete Saias are active tools for energetic unlocking. The marginalization of its dance expression reflects external prejudice against ritual happiness of African origin.

"Every skirt I twirl is a path I open."

"Joy is my greatest spell."

7. Pombagira Gypsy

Arquétipo: Liberdade e Prosperidade

The Gypsy Pombagira embodies the archetype of nomadism, reading destiny and prosperity achieved through detachment. The terreiro stories refer to gypsy clans, love suffering and the search for freedom — a narrative that dialogues directly with European literary representations about gypsy people.

Mediums who incorporate it are described as vain and magnetic, and their ritual performance focuses on material prosperity, opening financial paths and strengthening the autonomy of those who consult them.

Narrativa comum:

The gypsy image of Pombagiras reflects a spiritual tradition preserved intact since Europe.

O que costuma ser omitido:

Much of the gypsy aesthetic in Umbanda Rio comes from 19th century European literary romanticism (again, Carmen de Mérimée), which romanticized and exoticized real gypsy populations. Although the entity has its own strength and mystery, its visual appearance reproduces stereotypes about a people historically persecuted in Europe and Brazil.

"My destiny is written in the stars, but I'm the one holding the pen."

"Gold shines, but freedom shines."

8. Maria Navalha

Arquétipo: Malandragem e Rua

Maria Navalha is one of the Pombagiras most associated with the urban universe of Rio, linked especially to the Lapa neighborhood, in Rio de Janeiro — a territory historically marked by prostitution, bohemia and social marginalization at the beginning of the 20th century. Its archetype is that of survival, street smarts and the protection of women in vulnerable situations.

Mediums report a strong and combative personality, who protects those on the sidelines and does not tolerate abuse against women. Its ritual performance is sought in situations of risk, violence or the need to cut abusive ties.

Narrativa comum:

Maria Navalha is a purely "marginal" figure, associated with crime or violent Lapa.

O que costuma ser omitido:

Historical research on urban disciplinarization in Rio de Janeiro (1890-1930) shows that women like those who inspire Maria Navalha were criminalized by the police for the simple condition of existing autonomously outside of marriage. The entity represents the spiritual rehabilitation of women marginalized by republican patriarchal morality.

"My razor doesn't hurt, it frees."

"On the street I am law, on the hill I am queen."

9. Pombagira da Praia

Arquétipo: Emoção e Limpeza (Água)

The Pombagira da Praia symbolizes emotional fluidity, purification and renewal, with a strong symbolic connection to Iemanjá, the orixá of the sea in Afro-Brazilian cosmology. Its energy is described as light but deep, working especially to cleanse old hurts, traumas and emotional blockages.

Their ritual performance usually involves flushing baths, offerings linked to water and work aimed at emotional balance — a less combative and more therapeutic aspect within the spectrum of Pombagiras.

Narrativa comum:

The Pombagira da Praia is "less powerful" than the cemetery ones because it has a softer profile.

O que costuma ser omitido:

This view devalues ​​emotional and care work (generally associated with the female gender). In ritual reality, the fluidity and transmutation of the tides controlled by the Beach are tools of high therapeutic power and mental release.

"The sea takes what is no longer useful and brings what is new."

"My strength comes from the waves."

10. Maria Farrapo

Arquétipo: Sanidade e Caos (Mental)

Maria Farrapo represents the archetype of the reorganization of chaos and unfiltered reality. Terreiro narratives often involve loss of social status or episodes of deep mental suffering, finding in simplicity — or even in what society would call "madness" — an alternative form of truth and internal order.

Mediums describe a loyal manifestation, but straight to the point, without discursive minutiae. Its ritual performance is associated with mental stability, facing difficult realities and the courage to face what other entities could alleviate.

Narrativa comum:

Farrapo represents "madness" as a purely exotic or folkloric theme.

O que costuma ser omitido:

The madness in Farrapo's story carries real memories of women admitted to asylums or abandoned due to mental disorders in an era without adequate assistance. Treating this figure requires ethical caution, understanding it as a welcomer of deep psychic pain.

"In the midst of my chaos, I am the only order."

"I tear the cloth to show the soul."

11. Pombagira Menina

Arquétipo: Renovação e Inocência

The Pombagira Menina represents spiritual youth, curiosity and breaking rigid standards within the Pombagira pantheon itself. Terreiro reports associate her with spirits who disembodied young people or who preserve youthful energy even after passing away.

Mediums describe a sincere and captivating manifestation, with actions aimed at renewing hope and presenting unexpected solutions — often playful — to problems that seemed stuck.

Narrativa comum:

The Pombagira Menina is a fragile or ritually weightless figure relevant to serious works.

O que costuma ser omitido:

In Quimbanda, youthful and childish energies (such as erês) play a crucial role in the destructuring of complex spells precisely because they do not respond to adult rational logic. Its apparent lightness is a weapon for breaking ritual paradigms.

"I'm small in size, but giant in axé."

"I jokingly untie the knot you tightened."

12. Pombagira da Figueira

Arquétipo: Raiz e Ancestralidade (Terra)

The Pombagira da Figueira embodies the archetype of the ancient priestess and the deep connection with the land and ancestors. The fig tree, a tree historically associated in different cultures with spirits and magical practices, symbolically reinforces its connection with ancient herbal knowledge and pre-colonial cults.

Mediums report a strong connection with nature and ancestral knowledge in their manifestation. Its ritual performance is sought for spiritual support, rooting and recovery of connection with origins — whether family, cultural or spiritual.

Narrativa comum:

The Fig Tree represents a "pure" mystical tradition isolated from colonial influences.

O que costuma ser omitido:

Even entities with a strong connection to the land bear signs of syncretism with Portuguese popular Catholicism and indigenous pajelança. The search for a "pure" or pristine origin is an academic anachronism; Pombagira's wealth lies precisely in the hybrid crossing of these cultures under slave oppression.

"My roots are deep, no wind can knock me down."

"What I plant in the shade, I reap in the light."

Theoretical concepts and historical references

To support critical discussions and demystify the Pombagiras archetypes, academic research uses classic references from anthropology and social history:

  • Reginaldo Prandi — "Exu, de Mensageiro a Diabo: Sincretismo Católico e Demonização do Orixá Exu" (Revista USP, 2001) e "Mitologia dos Orixás" — Explica o processo histórico de demonização das entidades de esquerda pelas forças policiais e imprensa colonial no Rio de Janeiro.
  • Vagner Gonçalves da Silva — "Exu do Brasil: tropos de uma identidade afro-brasileira nos trópicos" (Revista de Antropologia, USP, 2012) — Analisa a incorporação do diabo cristão na figura do Exu brasileiro e a emergência de Pombagira.
  • Claudia Alexandre — "Exu-Mulher e o Matriarcado Nagô: sobre masculinização, demonização e tensões de gênero na formação dos candomblés" — Tese central que recupera a importância das sacerdotisas negras nos cultos a Exu, ofuscadas por análises androcêntricas.
  • Marlyse Meyer — Pesquisa folclórica sobre Maria Padilha, analisando a fusão de elementos cabalísticos, ciganos e ibéricos na formação da entidade, definida como a "geleia geral da magia brasileira".
  • Margareth Rago — "Do Cabaré ao Lar: a Utopia da Cidade Disciplinar, Brasil 1890–1930" — Obra referencial para compreender a regulação moral de prostitutas e mulheres da boemia no Rio de Janeiro, fornecendo o plano de fundo histórico para figuras como Maria Navalha.

Final considerations

The twelve Pombagiras gathered in this dossier do not exhaust the vastness of entities worshiped under this name in Brazilian terreiros — they merely represent a selection of the most widespread in Umbanda and Quimbanda literature aimed at the practicing public. What unites them all, beyond individual archetypes, is the fact that they carry, in their symbolic origin, memories of women who broke — by choice, by circumstance or by survival — with the social roles that were imposed on them.

The purely folkloric reading of these entities, focused on spectacular biography and aesthetics of seduction, tends to erase this denser social layer. The historical-anthropological reading, on the other hand, places the Pombagiras within concrete processes: the colonial and republican demonization of Afro-Brazilian religions, the police persecution of Macumbas at the beginning of the 20th century, and the systematic stigma placed on poor, black and socially marginalized women. Understanding the two layers — the faith lived in the terreiro and the historical process that shaped it — is what allows for truly complete informative material on the topic.
