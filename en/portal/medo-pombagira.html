Why Are Some People Afraid of Pombagiras?

An Investigation into the Fear of the Free Feminine by Alexia Melusine.

Few spiritual figures arouse reactions as intense as the Pombagiras.

Even people who have never attended a terreiro usually have an opinion about them. Some feel fascination, others feel curiosity, but many feel fear — a fear that often seems greater than their own knowledge on the subject.

And this raises an intriguing question:

"Why are Pombagiras so scary?"

The most common answer tends to be religious. They are said to be powerful, mysterious and difficult to understand entities. But perhaps this explanation is just the surface. Perhaps the true fear is not in the entity, but in what it symbolizes.

The Counterintuitive Hypothesis

Most people believe that they fear Pombagiras for spiritual reasons. But there is a deeper hypothesis: perhaps people are not afraid of the Pombagiras, but rather of the freedom they represent.

This idea may seem strange at first glance. However, when we look at the historical narratives associated with the Pombagiras, a pattern begins to emerge. They often appear linked to women considered:

  • Rebeldes e independentes;
  • Sedutoras;
  • Insubmissas;
  • Donas de suas próprias escolhas.

Historically, these characteristics have not always been celebrated; were often condemned. And this is precisely where the question becomes interesting.

The Feminine Who Doesn't Ask for Permission

Throughout much of human history, societies have constructed specific models to define what a "good woman" would be. She was expected to be obedient, discreet, restrained, dependent and morally controlled.

Women who deviated from this pattern often received negative labels, being called witches, sinners, sorcerers, lost or dangerous.

What draws attention is that many of these attributes reappear symbolically in the narratives about the Pombagiras. But a reversal occurs: what was previously used to condemn is now presented as force.

The Hidden History of Fear

The fear of the free feminine was not born with the Pombagiras — it is much older.

In medieval Europe, women accused of witchcraft were often healers, midwives, independent widows, or women living outside traditional structures. In Antiquity, several female deities associated with sexuality and autonomy were transformed into dangerous figures when new religious structures came to dominate certain regions.

The pattern is repeated countless times: the problem was rarely the woman, the problem was her autonomy. In this sense, the Pombagiras can be seen as symbolic heirs of a long history of marginalized feminines.

Carl Jung and the Collective Shadow

One of the most interesting contributions to understanding this phenomenon comes from Carl Gustav Jung. Jung proposed that individuals and societies have a shadow—composed of rejected aspects of the personality. But this concept can be expanded to entire groups: a society can also have a shadow.

In this context, the Pombagiras could represent elements that have been historically repressed: desire, freedom, feminine power, emotional expression and autonomy.

The more a culture represses a certain aspect, the scarier it tends to seem when it returns.

The Seduction Paradox

Few elements arouse as many contradictory interpretations as the sensuality associated with Pombagiras. It is common to find people who simultaneously feel attraction, fear, admiration and judgment.

This paradox is revealing, because fear does not always arise from rejection; sometimes it arises from attraction. What fascinates us can also scare us, especially when it questions values ​​that we learned from an early age.

The Feminine Fear of Pombagiras

There is another aspect that is rarely discussed: it is not always men who fear Pombagiras. Many women also feel discomfort in front of them. This happens because the feminine shadow doesn't just challenge masculine structures; she challenges anyone who has learned to repress parts of themselves.

A woman can admire the strength of a Pombagira, but at the same time feel afraid to express this strength in her own life. The conflict then stops being spiritual and becomes psychological.

LGBT+ people and identification with the Pombagiras

Perhaps one of the most fascinating phenomena is the strong identification that many LGBT+ people report with the Pombagiras. The simplistic explanation would be to say that they welcome marginalized people, but that doesn't explain everything. There is something deeper.

The Pombagiras represent the survival of what should have disappeared. They represent identities that refused erasure and the courage to exist despite disapproval. For this reason, many trans, transvestite and non-binary people find in them symbols of resistance and authenticity.

Victor Turner and the Crossroads Beings

Anthropologist Victor Turner studied liminal figures — beings that inhabit borders, that do not completely belong to one side or the other. Pombagiras often appear associated with crossroads, portals, streets and boundaries.

These locations have strong symbolic meaning. They are spaces of transformation, places where identities can change — and change often generates fear.

The Fear of Transformation

Perhaps this is the most profound interpretation. When someone claims to be afraid of a Pombagira, they may be expressing something bigger: fear of change. Because every transformation requires letting go of an old version of yourself, and that is rarely comfortable.

The symbolic presence of the Pombagiras often points to themes such as autonomy, personal truth, limits, repressed desires and reconstruction of identity. These processes can be liberating, but they can also be frightening.

The Question Few Ask

Perhaps the most important question is not "Why are Pombagiras scary?", but rather: "Why is freedom scary?"

Because when we look closely, we notice something curious. The characteristics most often associated with Pombagiras are exactly those that entire societies have tried to control for centuries: desire, independence, authenticity, courage and feminine power.

Maybe that's why they continue to arouse such intense reactions. Not because they only talk about spirituality, but because they touch on something much deeper. They force us to look at what has been repressed, at what has been silenced, at what remains hidden.

And maybe the real fear isn't the fear of Pombagira — maybe it's the fear of what happens when someone finally decides to be free.
